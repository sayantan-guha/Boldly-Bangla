# Bangladesh Copywriting Reference

*Source: Bonolota Express screenplay (Dec 2025 draft) — Samiul Bhuiyan & Ayman Asib Shadhin, Story by Ayman Asib Shadhin, Susmoy Sarker, Tanim Noor & Samiul Bhuiyan. Based on Humayun Ahmed's "Kichukkhon".*

> **IMPORTANT — USAGE RULE**: Pull from this file ONLY when explicitly told to write for a Bangladeshi audience, Bangladeshi platform, or Bangladesh-facing content. Do NOT mix these references into West Bengal / Kolkata copy. The registers are distinct.

---

## 1. LANGUAGE & REGISTER

### The Default Mode: Dhaka Banglish
Bangladeshi urban copy does not treat English mixing as occasional — it is the baseline register of the educated Dhaka middle class. Words that flow naturally without italicisation or quotation:

> উইয়ার্ড, ব্রেইন, স্টিম, গাইজ, সলিড, এগজ্যাক্টলি, ইনফরমেশন, অ্যাটেন্ড, অ্যাবসোলিউটলি, গর্জিয়াস, স্লো মোশন, পিওভি, ফ্রিজ ফ্রেম

Copy can use these without explanation. They signal cultural fluency, not laziness.

### Distinctly Bangladeshi Particles & Fillers
These do not exist the same way in Kolkata Bengali. Use them to make copy feel locally alive:

| Word/Phrase | Usage | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **ততো** | Emphatic/assertive particle | "এইটাই ততো মজার।" — conversational intimacy |
| **ক্যান** | "কেন" in spoken Dhaka dialect | "ক্যান এইটা করবে?" |
| **আমতা আমতা** | Hesitation, fumbling for words | Good for self-deprecating copy |
| **ভেজাল** | Trouble, complication | "ভেজালে পড়ে গেলাম।" |
| **খ্যাদানো** | To kick out / get rid of | Comic register |
| **হাউকাউ** | To make a fuss, to complain loudly | "হাউকাউ শুরু করে দেয়।" |
| **বেইজ্জতি** | Embarrassment / loss of face | Strong emotional word |
| **ক্লিয়ারেন্স** | Bureaucratic clearance | Used casually without translation |

### Name Patterns as Cultural Observation
Roshid's observation in the script is copywriting gold:
> "এক কালে সব মেয়ের নাম হতো ফুলের নামে— জুঁই, জেসমিন, রোজিনা, চামেলী। আর ছেলেদের জন্য ছিল ফল— ডালিম, আপেল, আনার। এখন শুরু হয়েছে নদী-নালার যুগ।"

**Bangladesh rivers as women's names**: সুরমা, যমুনা, মেঘনা, পদ্মা — these carry feminine grace AND geographic identity. Extremely specific to Bangladesh (these rivers flow through Bangladesh, not Kolkata).

## 2. SOCIAL & INSTITUTIONAL REFERENCES

### The BUET Hierarchy
In Bangladesh, BUET (Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology) rank is the ultimate middle-class status marker — not rank in general. Being "BUET-তে তেরোতম" (13th in BUET) is biographical glory. Copy targeting educated Bangladeshi families can use this shorthand.

> Equivalent: BUET rank > DU admission > everything else.
> Note: Do NOT use "IIT" — that's India. BUET is Bangladesh's IIT.

### Transport Specifics
- **CNG (গেটবিহীন সিএনজি)** — the auto-rickshaw without doors. Quintessentially Bangladeshi urban transport. "একটা গেটবিহীন সিএনজি" is immediately visualised by any Dhaka reader.
- **Kamalapur Railway Station** — বাংলাদেশের মূল রেলস্টেশন। Not Howrah, not Chittagong Central. এটা Dhaka.
- **উত্তরবঙ্গের ট্রেন** — the train going north from Dhaka; carries specific middle-class associations.

### Baksheesh Culture (layered payments)
The coolie scene gives a perfect copywriting structure — each layer of expectation:
1. ব্যাগ টানার বিল
2. ট্রলির চার্জ
3. বখশিস
4. (satirically) ঈদের বোনাস? বৈশাখী ভাতা?

**বৈশাখী ভাতা** is SPECIFICALLY Bangladeshi — the festival allowance paid by employers at Poila Boisakh. In West Bengal, this does not exist as a formal labor concept. Using it in copy signals Bangladeshi economic reality.

### VIP Culture
Blocking roads for ministers is a lived Bangladeshi experience. The traffic jam because of a VIP convoy ("যে জ্যাম! কোন নাকি ভিআইপি আসতেছিল স্টেশনে— ওর জন্য পুরা রাস্তা আটকায় রাখসে") is NOT satirical in Bangladesh — it is daily life. Copy that touches this will land with immediate recognition and dark humor.

### The Grammar Book
**Chowdhury & Hossain Advanced Learners'** — the definitive Bangladeshi English grammar book. Every Bangladeshi student knows the "He ran fast lest he should miss the train" sentence. This is NOT Wren & Martin (Indian). Powerful nostalgia trigger for Bangladeshi audiences.

## 3. RELIGIOUS & SUPERSTITIOUS TEXTURE

Islam is woven naturally into Bangladeshi middle-class speech and behavior — not performed, not apologized for:

- **তসবিহ** (prayer beads) as a prop of a superstitious older woman
- **ফজরের ওয়াক্ত** — Fajr prayer time used as a marker for when ghosts roam ("ফজরের ওয়াক্ত না হওয়া পর্যন্ত ভূত-প্রেতরা ঘুরাঘুরি করে")
- **স্লামালিকুম** — casual greeting form, natural in dialogue
- **হায়াত কমে যায়** — "your lifespan decreases" — said about speaking one's age aloud. An everyday superstition.

**Copywriting implication**: In Bangladesh, religion and superstition coexist with modernity without contradiction. A character can be devout AND code-switch into English. Copy that acknowledges this complexity feels true.

## 4. CLASS & POLITICAL DYNAMICS

### The Train as Class Metaphor (Bangladesh version)
Roshid's observation: *"ক্লাস ডিভিশন এইখানেও আছে, কিন্তু সেইটা উপরে আর নিচে না, সামনে আর পিছনে। তাছাড়া এক ঘর থেকে আরেক ঘরে যাওয়ার দরজা এইখানে সবসময়ই খোলা।"*

The Bangladeshi democratic ideal — anyone can move through. A socialist metaphor with local specificity.

### Political Humor — Sharper in Bangladesh
Bangladesh copy can carry sharper political edge than Kolkata copy. The Bangladeshi middle class has an acute, lived relationship with political corruption, enforced disappearances (Mohanagar 2), and VIP impunity.

> "উত্তরবঙ্গের ট্রেন আমাদের দেশের মানুষের পলিটিকাল ম্যাচিওরিটির মতো— সবসময়ই একটু দেরি করে আসে।"

This kind of political line works in Bangladesh because the audience shares the fatigue. Don't soften it. The irony lands harder when left dry.

### Land = Iman
> "জমি হইল ঈমানের মতো— একবার ফসকায় গেলে ফিরা পাওয়া কঠিন।"

In Bangladesh, land is not just property — it is identity, family honor, and faith combined. Land disputes have a moral-religious weight that is specific to Bangladesh's rural-to-urban migration pattern. Copy that touches this will resonate with any Bangladeshi who has family land.

## 5. FAMILY DYNAMICS

### Mother-Son: The Superstitious Controller
Sajeda and Ashhab is a perfect Bangladeshi archetype:
- Mother suspects son's smoking but frames it as moral failure
- AC room is seen as a plot to isolate her from social contact
- Ghosts, Fajr timing, husband comparisons — all weaponized conversationally
- Son escapes by saying "আমি মদ খাইতে যাই, আম্মা" — knowing she won't believe it

**Copywriting implication**: Bangladeshi mother-son copy hits differently from Kolkata. More confrontational, more comic, more openly affectionate-through-conflict. The mother is formidable, not gentle.

### Husband-Wife: The Aziz-Afia Dynamic
- Husband wants to control logistics (window open/closed)
- Wife deploys weapons of vulnerability (pregnancy, miscarriages, implied sacrifice)
- Daughter (Nitu) covers her ears and plays mobile games — the third-generation cope
- The fight is loud, direct, ending in unresolved standoff

**Copywriting implication**: Bangladesh copy can portray marital conflict more bluntly than Kolkata. The wit is in the weapons used, not in subtlety. The humor is not warm-ironic — it is sharp-affectionate.

### The Guardian Culture
Babu Mama travelling as Ruby's official guardian signals Bangladesh's social structure where young women still require male chaperones for inter-city travel in conservative family contexts. This coexists with Chitra's radical independence. Both are true simultaneously.

## 7. RHYTHM AND COPY TECHNIQUES (Bangladesh-specific)

### The Narrator Voice — Modern Dhaka
The Bonolota Express narrator establishes the ideal voice for modern Bangladeshi OTT copy:
- Starts with a philosophical observation, immediately deflates it with humor
- Uses "আই মিন" and "এগজ্যাক্টলি" mid-Bengali sentence without apology
- Refers to the audience as "আপনারা" — formal but warm
- Self-corrects mid-sentence ("সরি, সরি, সরি, মাই মিস্টেক")
- Builds absurdist scenarios with deadpan delivery

> Template: [Philosophical setup] → [Specific/absurd example] → [Comic deflation] → [Direct address to audience]

### The "দুইটার একটাও" Structure
Roshid's line: "দুইটার একটাও সম্ভব না। প্রথমত, আমি সিগারেটে টান না দিয়ে থাকতে পারি না। আর দ্বিতীয়ত..."

**Use for**: setting up expectations and then systematically eliminating them. Works for product copy, feature ads, show promotion.

### The River-Sisters Joke Template
Boshir's line: "সুরমা আর যমুনার পর আরেকটা বোন থাকলে পরে তার নাম দেওয়া লাগত কুশিয়ারা।"

**Structure**: [Observed pattern] → [Logical extension to absurdity]. Works brilliantly for Bangladeshi audiences who will catch Kushi River immediately. Quick, visual, culturally precise.

## 8. QUICK REFERENCE: WHAT BANGLADESH COPY DOES DIFFERENTLY

| Dimension | Bangladesh | West Bengal |
|---|---|---|
| **Code-switching** | Default register; heavy | Selective; ironic |
| **Political humor** | Sharp, lived, unironic | Satirical, distant |
| **Religion in copy** | Natural texture | Generally avoided |
| **Mother archetype** | Formidable, confrontational | Gentle, nostalgic |
| **Land references** | Moral/faith weight | Property/legal weight |
| **Music icons** | Ayub Bachchu, Arthohin | Anupam Roy, Rupam Islam |
| **Academic pinnacle** | BUET | Presidency / JU |
| **Festival pay** | বৈশাখী ভাতা (real concept) | Not applicable |
| **Train culture** | Dhaka → north; CNG to station | Howrah; yellow taxi |
| **Marital conflict** | Direct, comic, weaponised | Quiet, ironic, restrained |
